### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act [3] was legislation passed in South Africa, which was part of the Apartheid system [8]. It was a law that aimed to create separate areas for people from different racial groups. 

In point of fact, except for a few areas, most of South Africa was already divided into separate areas [3]. However, this was different in Cape Town as the legislation played havoc with the lives of ordinary people. The government worked on the theory that people tended to anticipate the authorities' wishes [4].

The Act stated that all people had to be properly classified, or "colour coded" to give effect to their plans [8]. It led to the Group Areas Board carrying out a survey to classify District Six residents into various racial groups, including Coloured, Indian, and White. The classification process aimed to re-distribute inhabitants according to these categories.

For people in households with mixed ancestry like the author's family (Rassool), this created problems [10]. The rules did not take into account the complexities of families like theirs and made it difficult for them to adjust to their new classifications. This was because, under Apartheid law, Indians were required to live in their own restricted area outside the city [10].

The Act effectively destroyed District Six as a functioning community space where people from different ethnic groups came together. It is mentioned that the roads in District Six were 'mere mud tracks' with little street lighting; however, there were pleasant schools in the area, known locally as "location palaces," which may have attracted some prospective residents to move in [13].

As a result of the Act and the Apartheid regime in general, hundreds of thousands of people had their lives devastated [14]. Many lost their homes, livelihoods, or even access to healthcare. The author notes that it was an 'obscene social experiment' intended to destroy community life as much as possible.

In terms of District Six specifically, the Group Areas Act and its implementation contributed significantly to the decline of the area's infrastructure and economy [9]. It started early with the closure of shops in Hanover Street, the main artery of the area, in the 1950s.

Gandhi remained in South Africa until 1914, fighting for the rights of Indians in South Africa and taking their case to Britain.

### q39 — What was District Six like as a neighbourhood before the forced removals?

Before the forced removals, District Six was described as "a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighbourhood of Cape Town, South Africa" [1]. It was home to several thousand residents, including families such as the Rassools and Gools [1].

The author Joe Rassool recalled that Caledon Street ran from Corporation Street straight through to Clifton Street, but got "tattier along the way" [3]. The community had a mix of people from different racial groups living together, with Indians being forced to live in designated areas outside the city.

The neighbourhood was known for its diversity and vibrancy. The author mentions that tap dancers would perform on the sidewalks without hope of achieving fame or fortune [10]. Residents, particularly children like Joe Rassool himself, were encouraged to explore their surroundings and engage with others from different cultural backgrounds [6].

A community of freed slaves from America and the West Indies thrived in District Six, some achieving great position and wealth [18]. However, when the apartheid era evictions began, these individuals were expelled along with other residents.

The neighbourhood was largely mixed-income, with many people living in close proximity to wealthy areas. Joe Rassool's family lived on Caledon Street, near more affluent areas of Cape Town. They eventually moved to a large house at 95 Caledon Street [15].

Overall, District Six before the forced removals was a cosmopolitan area filled with diversity and culture, but ultimately subject to forced removal under apartheid laws.

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

The Unity Movement held firmly to the belief that its ideas and methods of struggle would triumph in the end (Source [8]). The primary weapon used by the movement against apartheid institutions was the boycott (Sources [2], [8]). 

However, the effectiveness of the boycott is questioned in certain contexts. For instance, boycotting the Group Areas Act did not pose problems for the propertyless or homeless but created issues for those who owned property (Source [12]). Also, there were cases where members of the "Movement" simply ignored the injunction to boycott (Source [12]).

In the general elections mentioned in Source [18] and again in a different context as part of an episode in Source [16], The Communist Party's decision to break ranks with the Unity Movement and put up its candidates caused the Unity Movement to boycott the election. If "Coloured" voters insisted on voting, they were asked to spoil their ballot by writing: “For Full Democratic Rights” across the ballot slip (Source [18]).
