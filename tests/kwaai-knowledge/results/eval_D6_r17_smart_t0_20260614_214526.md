In the case of District Six, a vibrant and multicultural neighborhood in Cape Town, the Group Areas Act had a devastating impact. The government declared that District Six would be reclassified as a "White" area, which meant that its residents, who were primarily Coloured (mixed-race) people, would have to leave their homes.

The effects of the Group Areas Act on District Six were severe:

* Residents were forced to sell their properties at low prices or accept minimal compensation from the government.
* Many families were split apart as some members were classified as "Indian" and others as "Malay", creating a complex and difficult situation for those with mixed ancestry.
* The community was torn apart, with many residents being forcibly removed from their homes and relocated to other areas on the outskirts of Cape Town.

The Group Areas Act had a profound impact on District Six, leading to the displacement of thousands of people and the destruction of the community's social fabric. The act is widely regarded as one of the most egregious examples of apartheid-era forced removals in South Africa.

### q39 — What was District Six like as a neighbourhood before the forced removals?

According to [1], District Six was a "vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighbourhood of Cape Town, South Africa" that was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents. It was described as having "more life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care than many other places one could mention" ([3]).

The neighborhood was known for its cosmopolitan atmosphere, with people from different cultures and backgrounds living together. The author recalls seeing white lady doctors who preferred to live in District Six despite the poverty and squalor surrounding them ([10]). The area had a mix of shops, including the British Bioscope, which showed films from various studios, and the National Theatre in William Street, which showed mainly MGM films ([10]).

The neighborhood was also home to many eccentric characters who added to its unique flavor. For example, there were tap-dancing groups that performed on the sidewalks without any hope of achieving fame or fortune ([13]). The author remembers Abdul coming home with a pocket full of pennies and inviting him to join in a foray into downtown where they would ask for pennies from passersby ([13]).

Overall, District Six was described as a lively and vibrant neighborhood that was full of life and energy. However, it was also plagued by poverty and squalor, which would eventually lead to its forced removals under the Group Areas Act in the 1970s and 1980s.

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

The Unity Movement's boycott policy is described in excerpt [10]. According to this source, the boycott weapon was considered ineffective against Apartheid. The Movement realized that boycotting the Group Areas Act would mean refusing to cooperate with the Group Areas Boards without any guarantee that it would stop the implementation of the law.

Excerpt [10] states: "To boycott the Group Areas Act would mean to refuse to cooperate with the Group Areas Boards without having any guarantee that this would stop the implementation of the law. To the propertiless, homeless or down-and-out people, boycotting the Group Areas Act did not create any problems, but to those who owned property it was a different matter."

Additionally, excerpt [18] mentions that the Unity Movement's stand was to boycott the election, but if the "Coloured" voters insisted on voting they were asked to spoil their ballot by writing: “For Full Democratic Rights” across the ballot slip.

It appears that the Unity Movement's boycott policy was not a blanket refusal to participate in institutions or laws, but rather a strategic decision to use boycotts as a means of protest and to highlight the injustices of Apartheid.
