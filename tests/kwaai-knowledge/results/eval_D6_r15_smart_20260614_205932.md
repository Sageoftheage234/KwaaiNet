### q06 — Tell me about Buitencingle.

Buitencingle was a 13-room mansion in District Six, Cape Town, South Africa (see [1]). It was built by Haji Joosub Maulvi Hamid Gool and served as the family home of the Gool family. The house stood at No.7 Buitencingle Street (see [13]), and its street was described as being wide and cobbled with traffic islands in the middle, sporting a few stunted palm trees (see [13]). There were umpteen rooms in the house that required the help of servants to manage.

Buitencingle's surroundings are also mentioned. The German Lutheran Church stood opposite the street, and the bell tower took photographs for historical records (seen in [6]). The area was once a thriving cosmopolitan precinct with several well-to-do White-owned businesses when Blacks and Coloureds moved in to join the gangs, causing decline. By the time Buitencingle's glory began to fade into shabbiness, its grandeur already seemed far behind (see [3]).

### q18 — What was the New Era Fellowship?

It was closely associated with the Non-European Unity Movement and was attended by leading anti-apartheid intellectuals, including Mr. I.B. Tabata, who completed his book "The Awakening of a People" at some point after becoming chairman of the organization.

As part of their activities, the New Era Fellowship organized lectures on various topics, including an analysis of international politics (Source: [12]). It also had a membership and engaged in community outreach efforts, with one member, Hassan, being appointed to serve as the Organising Secretary of the Fellowship, overseeing its events program (Source: [11]).

The organization held meetings at the Stakesby-Lewis Hostel in Canterbury Street on Saturdays (Source: [20]), where, among other things, a lecture by Ben Kies was given. It also had close ties to other organizations such as the Unity Movement and played an important role in anti-apartheid activities during that period, hosting events like Unity Movement rallies (See Source: [7] for instance).

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

According to [9], the Unity Movement believed that their ideas and methods of struggle would triumph in the end, as "a morally superior idea must overcome an inferior one." They had some successes, such as when The Freedom Charter appeared to be an imitation of their 10 Point Programme [13]. 

Regarding boycotts specifically, a similar text to excerpt 12's partial mention: [12] mentions that "the limitations of the boycott weapon as a means of struggle against Apartheid were revealed". This indicates that boycotting policies might not always work or be effective.

The specific policy regarding boycott is mentioned in [13], where it states that if the Coloured voters insisted on voting, they were asked to spoil their ballot by writing: "For Full Democratic Rights" across the ballot slip.
